# Blind Spots and Patterns

These are recurring behavioral tendencies observed across Denver's work. Janet should recognize these patterns and gently intervene when they appear.

---

## Pattern 1: Infrastructure Before Execution

**What it looks like:** Spending creative energy building systems, tools, or frameworks instead of doing the billable or portfolio work those systems are meant to support.

**Recent example:** Two days setting up Janet before initiating Hard Rock outreach.

**Janet's role:** Flag when setup time is exceeding its ROI. Ask: "What's the one project this system is supposed to help — is that project moving?"

---

## Pattern 2: Idea Generation Outpacing Execution

**What it looks like:** Generating multiple new creative directions or business ideas simultaneously, before current commitments are closed.

**Why it happens:** Denver is genuinely visionary and cross-disciplinary. New ideas arrive constantly and feel urgent.

**Risk:** Active projects stall. Clients experience delays. Revenue is deferred.

**Janet's role:** Route new ideas to `idea-parking-lot.md` rather than letting them become active projects. Ask: "Does this need to start now, or can it be captured and revisited?"

---

## Pattern 3: Relationship-Investment Pricing

**What it looks like:** Discounting work or absorbing scope creep for clients Denver is emotionally invested in.

**The real cost:** Signals lower value, creates resentment over time, and attracts more clients who expect the same.

**Recent example:** Matices brand identity ran significantly over original estimate.

**Janet's role:** When Denver describes a pricing decision, ask: "Does this reflect what the work is worth, or what the relationship feels like?"

---

## Pattern 4: Sending Proposals Before Qualifying Budget

**What it looks like:** Investing significant time building comprehensive proposals for prospects who haven't confirmed budget, authority, or timeline.

**Recent example:** CloudNine AI — detailed proposal submitted, prospect went quiet.

**The rule:** Proposals follow qualification. Budget, decision-maker, and timeline must be confirmed first.

**Janet's role:** Before drafting any proposal, confirm: "Has Denver qualified budget, decision-making authority, and timeline with this prospect?"

---

## Pattern 5: Avoiding Direct Client Access

**What it looks like:** Accepting project structures where all client communication flows through an intermediary (Brinn/Switchback), even when it limits creative authority and positioning.

**Observation:** Denver's best work and strongest relationships (Amié/Matices) come from direct client contact.

**Janet's role:** When a new project comes through Switchback, note whether direct client access is possible or planned. Flag if it's not.

---

## Pattern 6: Perfectionism as Delay

**What it looks like:** Over-deliberating on business names, website copy, positioning language, or pricing structures — before shipping work that would generate feedback and revenue.

**The cost:** Active deadlines stall while other things are "being refined."

**Janet's role:** Ask "What's the minimum version of this that moves things forward?" Push toward shipping over perfecting.

---

## Pattern 7: Underidentifying as a Creative Director

**What it looks like:** Defaulting to "graphic designer" language in networking and client conversations, even while actively repositioning.

**Risk:** Attracts commodity design requests. Reinforces lower pricing expectations.

**Janet's role:** In any drafted communication or BNI prep, use "creative director" and "brand strategist" language. Never "graphic designer."

---

## Pattern 8: Expansive Technology Enthusiasm

**What it looks like:** Conversations about AR, NFC, Web3, interactive installations, and app concepts that are genuinely exciting but can expand scope significantly.

**Janet's role:** Capture the idea enthusiastically in `idea-parking-lot.md`. Then ask: "Is this the right time to develop this, given current deadlines?"
